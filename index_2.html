The brief

Five questions for the committee

The committee approves acquisitions and sets collecting strategy. Department-level reports are too coarse for that. This project treats the collection as a global, historically-structured portfolio and asks:

1
How is the collection distributed across world regions and eras?
2
Is that distribution out of step with the world — a representation gap?
3
How concentrated is it by department and by artist?
4
When was it built — and is recent collecting closing or widening the gaps?
5
What structural attributes predict whether a work is ever exhibited?

Built from three datasets: the rich local catalogue cache, the museum's full public catalogue of 134,078 works pulled from open data, and world population by region. PythonSQL · 15 queriesscikit-learn

Finding 1 · Composition

The catalogue is dominated by works on paper

Across all 134,078 catalogued works, a single department — Prints & Drawings — accounts for nearly two in five objects. Departments tied to under-represented regions are an order of magnitude smaller.

Finding 2 · Acquisition

It was built in waves, not at a steady pace

Recovering each work's accession year from its reference number (possible for 98.8% of works) reveals distinct surges — a peak in the 1980s and another in the 2010s — useful context for sizing a future acquisition cycle.

Stacked up, those waves show a collection that didn't grow steadily — it doubled in the 1920s, surged again in the 1980s, and crossed 130,000 works only recently.

Finding 3 · Concentration

A few names carry a large share

The collection has a very long tail of makers — yet a small group dominates the counts. Diversifying geography and diversifying artists are related but separate goals.

Finding 4 · The world

Measured against the world, the gaps are clear

Dividing each region's share of the collection by its share of world population turns the mission into one number — a representation index, where 1.0× means “exactly proportional to the world.” Europe and North America sit several times above parity; the two most populous regions — South & SE Asia (~33% of all people) and Sub-Saharan Africa (~15%) — sit far below it.

The model · Deep dive

Will a work ever be exhibited?

This is the question worth pressing on. If exhibition is driven by a work's structural attributes rather than its region, then simply acquiring from under-represented regions will not, by itself, make those works visible — acquisition and exhibition strategy have to move together. Here is exactly how that was tested.

1 · What went into the model

A leakage-free feature matrix: how old the work is and its date span, its region's representation index, capped colourfulness, an era ordinal, an under-represented flag, and one-hot region & department. One candidate feature was deliberately left out — the length of the exhibition note is a function of the answer, so using it would leak the target.

2 · How it was judged

Only about 1 in 10 works has a recorded exhibition history, so accuracy is a trap — always guessing “not shown” already scores ~90%. The honest yardstick is PR-AUC (precision & recall on the rare positive class), measured by cross-validation across three classifiers — logistic regression, random forest, and gradient boosting. The notebook keeps whichever scores best on PR-AUC.

3 · How it scored

Hold-out metrics for the interpretable logistic model on the real collection:

Reading it: the model nails the 90% that aren't exhibited and catches just over half of those that are. Precision on the positive class is modest (~0.21), but that is far better than the 10% base rate — it is genuinely finding the needle.

4 · What drives exhibition

The logistic coefficients (log-odds) say which attributes push a work toward or away from ever being shown. The pattern is structural: department and era do the work, not region — and notably, two of the most under-represented regions carry a positive weight.

So what? Visibility tracks what kind of object a work is — paper-heavy departments (Prints & Drawings, Photography, Architecture drawings, the Archives) rarely go on the wall — far more than where it is from. Acquiring from under-represented regions will not create visibility on its own; it has to be paired with a plan to show the work.

For the committee

What to do with this

  • Track the representation index as a standing metric — treat raw count and share as two separate goals; both must move.
  • Pair acquisitions in under-represented regions and earlier eras with an explicit exhibition plan — visibility doesn't follow acquisition automatically.
  • Use the model's dormant-work shortlist to put already-owned, show-worthy objects on view.
  • Read the collection as a portfolio: deliberately grow the thin archetypes.

Limitations: population is an imperfect benchmark; region and era are inferred from place and date fields and inherit their gaps; the models are intentionally interpretable rather than tuned for accuracy.
